Overview

Water Bear Lab is a laboratory-based VR experience centered on water bears, developed in collaboration with the Senckenberg Naturmuseum in Frankfurt as an educational application. Users step into a virtual lab and perform a set of test interactions on a water bear specimen, exposing it to extreme conditions and observing how the system responds within defined limits.

The experience is designed to be forgiving and exploratory. Tests can be completed in any order, progress is always recoverable, and incorrect or experimental actions never permanently break the system. The goal was to encourage curiosity instead of the fear of "doing it wrong", so that visitors feel free to try, fail, and try again.

Research

The project started with field research at the museum. We spent time on site observing visitors, watching how they moved through the space and what caught their attention, and used those observations to define our audience and target group.

Based on that, we chose three candidate topics for the final experience: Armadillos & Pangolins, Penguins and Water Bears. Across several further rounds of research we tested each one, dropped two of them, and kept tardigrades (water bears) as the strongest direction. By the end of the process we had gone deep enough into the subject to feel almost like experts on it.

To get there, we worked through several project stages and applied a wide range of design research methods. These included, among others:

  • On-site observation of museum visitors and their behaviour.
  • Topic research into each of the three candidate themes.
  • "Why, why, why" and "how" questioning to reach the underlying needs.
  • Emotional journey mapping.
  • Personas.
  • Affinity maps.

And many more methods beyond the ones listed here. Together they helped us surface the potential problems of each topic, its strengths and weaknesses, and the opportunities and interesting angles it offered. That comparison is what ultimately guided the final choice of tardigrades.

The full research process, every method, all of the research results, the presentations and more is documented in the Documentation Appendix.

What the experience does

At its core, the experience lets visitors experiment on a water bear specimen in a safe, controlled virtual lab and watch the consequences play out in real time.

  • A set of lab test interactions that expose a water bear specimen to extreme conditions.
  • Real-time system responses kept within defined, safe limits.
  • Non-linear flow, so tests can be completed in any order.
  • Recoverable progress, so exploratory or incorrect actions never break the experience.

My role

I worked as the Technical Artist on the project, the bridge between the programmer, the designer and the experience itself. My responsibilities spanned both the engineering and the visual sides of production:

  • Implementing and optimizing the interactions, editing and writing the game logic.
  • Creating the 3D models, VFX and shaders in Maya, Substance Painter and Unity itself.
  • Defining and unifying the overall look of the experience.
  • Translating design intent into a performant, polished result on standalone hardware.

Outcome

Testing of the project returned mostly positive results. Users responded well to the visual style, the narrative, the usability and the fun, engaging nature of the interactions, and they appreciated the unusual concept behind the experience. Overall the feedback confirmed that the lab format and the choice of tardigrades resonated with visitors and made an unfamiliar topic approachable and memorable.
